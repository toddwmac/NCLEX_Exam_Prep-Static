Respiratory System

High Priority ABG Analysis Interventions

NCLEX Focus

Respiratory topics are fundamental to NCLEX success because:

  • Airway management is the first priority in patient care
  • Respiratory distress requires rapid assessment and intervention
  • Oxygenation affects all body systems
  • Many interventions require critical thinking and clinical judgment

Key Testing Areas:

  • Assessment of respiratory status and work of breathing
  • Interpretation of arterial blood gases (ABGs)
  • Management of oxygen delivery devices
  • Care of patients with chronic respiratory conditions

Quick Reference Summary

Priority Assessments
  • Respiratory rate & pattern
  • Breath sounds & work of breathing
  • SpO2 & ABG values
  • Sputum characteristics
Critical Lab Values
  • PaO2 < 60 mmHg
  • PaCO2 > 50 mmHg
  • pH < 7.35 or > 7.45
  • SpO2 < 90%
Key Medications
  • Bronchodilators
  • Corticosteroids
  • Mucolytics
  • Antibiotics

ABG Interpretation Guide

Condition pH PaCO2 HCO3 Clinical Presentation
Respiratory Acidosis < 7.35 ↑ Normal/↑ Drowsiness, confusion, headache
Respiratory Alkalosis > 7.45 ↓ Normal/↓ Lightheadedness, tingling, tetany
Metabolic Acidosis < 7.35 Normal/↓ ↓ Kussmaul breathing, weakness

NCLEX Success Mnemonics

COPD vs. Asthma (COLD vs. WARM)
  • Chronic symptoms vs. Wheezing
  • Older adults vs. Allergies common
  • Limited reversibility vs. Reversible
  • Daily symptoms vs. Mostly episodic
ABG Steps (ROME)
  • Respiratory or Metabolic?
  • Opposites (pH and primary disorder)
  • Match (compensation)
  • Evaluate degree of compensation

Clinical Application

Question: A patient with COPD has the following ABG values: pH 7.30, PaCO2 55 mmHg, HCO3 28 mEq/L, PaO2 58 mmHg. Which interpretation is correct?

  1. Uncompensated respiratory acidosis
  2. Partially compensated respiratory acidosis
  3. Metabolic acidosis with respiratory compensation
  4. Mixed acid-base disorder
Answer: B - Partially compensated respiratory acidosis

Rationale: The elevated PaCO2 indicates respiratory acidosis (primary disorder). The slightly elevated HCO3 shows partial metabolic compensation. This pattern is common in COPD patients.

Neurological System

High Priority Neuro Assessment Critical Care

NCLEX Focus

Neurological assessment is crucial for NCLEX because:

  • Changes in neurological status can indicate life-threatening conditions
  • Early recognition of deterioration is essential for patient outcomes
  • Neurological assessment requires systematic approach and critical thinking
  • Many medications affect neurological function

Key Testing Areas:

  • Glasgow Coma Scale assessment
  • Stroke assessment and interventions
  • Increased intracranial pressure management
  • Seizure precautions and management

Quick Reference Summary

Priority Assessments
  • Level of consciousness
  • Pupillary response
  • Motor strength
  • Cranial nerve function
Warning Signs
  • Decreased LOC
  • Unequal pupils
  • New onset seizures
  • Severe headache
Key Medications
  • Anticonvulsants
  • Mannitol
  • tPA
  • Antipsychotics

Stroke Assessment (FAST)

FAST Assessment
  • Facial drooping
  • Arm weakness
  • Speech difficulties
  • Time to call emergency services
Additional Signs
  • Sudden confusion
  • Vision problems
  • Severe headache
  • Balance problems

Neurological Pharmacology

Drug Class Common Drugs Key Nursing Considerations Priority Side Effects
Anticonvulsants phenytoin, levetiracetam Monitor drug levels, assess gait Drowsiness, ataxia, gingival hyperplasia
Osmotic Diuretics mannitol Monitor ICP, fluid balance Electrolyte imbalance, dehydration
Thrombolytics alteplase (tPA) Time window critical, monitor for bleeding Hemorrhage, hypotension

Clinical Application

Question: A patient presents with sudden onset of right-sided weakness and aphasia. Which intervention should the nurse implement first?

  1. Obtain CT scan
  2. Check blood glucose
  3. Administer aspirin
  4. Complete NIH Stroke Scale
Answer: B - Check blood glucose

Rationale: Hypoglycemia can mimic stroke symptoms. Blood glucose must be checked before administering tPA or other interventions to rule out stroke mimics.

  • Minimize stimulation
  • Monitor vital signs
  • Stable ICP readings
  • Improved LOC

Risk for Injury
  • Implement seizure precautions
  • Maintain patent airway
  • Monitor oxygen saturation
  • Prevent Valsalva maneuver
  • No seizure activity
  • Adequate oxygenation
  • No complications

Gastrointestinal System

GI Assessment Nutrition Medications

NCLEX Focus

Gastrointestinal disorders are important for NCLEX because:

  • Common conditions affect quality of life and recovery
  • Nutritional status impacts all body systems
  • Many medications affect GI function
  • Early recognition prevents serious complications

Key Testing Areas:

  • Nutritional assessment and support
  • Fluid and electrolyte balance
  • GI bleeding management
  • Post-operative care

Quick Reference Summary

Priority Assessments
  • Bowel sounds
  • Abdominal pain
  • Nutritional status
  • Output characteristics
Warning Signs
  • Acute abdominal pain
  • GI bleeding
  • Severe nausea/vomiting
  • Changes in output
Key Medications
  • Antacids
  • Antiemetics
  • Laxatives
  • Antidiarrheals
